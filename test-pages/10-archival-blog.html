Introduction

Vannevar Bush's 1945 essay "As We May Think" proposed a device called the memex — a mechanized desk that would allow a researcher to store, retrieve, and link documents through associative trails.1 The essay, published in The Atlantic Monthly, has since become one of the most cited texts in information science.

This article traces the memex concept from its origins through its influence on hypertext, personal knowledge management, and contemporary digital tools. It argues that while the memex is often treated as a prophecy of the World Wide Web, its actual design principles point toward something quite different: a private, cumulative, and deeply personal instrument for thought.

Origins of the memex

Bush wrote "As We May Think" during the final months of World War II, when he directed the U.S. Office of Scientific Research and Development. The essay responded to a specific problem: the growing volume of scientific literature had outstripped any individual's capacity to navigate it.2

The memex was imagined as a desk with translucent screens, a keyboard, and a vast store of microfilm. A researcher could call up any document, annotate it, and — crucially — create trails linking one document to another. These trails could then be shared with colleagues, creating a new form of scholarly communication.

"Wholly new forms of encyclopedias will appear, ready-made with a mesh of associative trails running through them." — Vannevar Bush, 1945

Influence on hypertext

Ted Nelson, who coined the term "hypertext" in 1965, acknowledged Bush as a direct inspiration.3 Nelson's Project Xanadu extended the memex concept into a networked system with bidirectional links, transclusion, and version tracking.

Douglas Engelbart, whose 1968 "Mother of All Demos" introduced the mouse, windowed interfaces, and collaborative document editing, also cited Bush.4 Both Nelson and Engelbart, however, diverged from Bush in a critical way: they envisioned shared, public systems rather than personal archives.

The Zettelkasten parallel

Running in parallel to these developments, the German sociologist Niklas Luhmann developed his Zettelkasten (slip-box) method from the 1960s onward.5 Luhmann's system of approximately 90,000 interlinked index cards bears a striking resemblance to Bush's memex, though there is no evidence Luhmann was aware of the essay.

Both systems share key properties: items are individually addressable, connections between items are explicit and navigable, and the system grows by accretion rather than reorganization. The Zettelkasten has experienced a revival in the 2020s through digital tools such as Obsidian, Roam Research, and Logseq.

Digital reincarnations

The memex metaphor has been invoked by designers of numerous digital tools. The most direct descendants include:

  • Apple's HyperCard (1987) — card-based linking system6
  • The World Wide Web (1991) — though notably lacking bidirectional links
  • Ward Cunningham's WikiWikiWeb (1995) — collaborative, editable hypertext
  • Are.na (2014) — visual bookmarking and associative linking
  • Obsidian (2020) — local-first, graph-based note-taking

Each of these tools embodies some aspect of Bush's vision while departing from others. The Web, for instance, realized the scale Bush imagined but abandoned the personal, cumulative character of the memex. Tools like Obsidian restore the personal dimension but sacrifice the sharing mechanism.

Limits of the metaphor

It is worth noting what the memex was not. Bush did not anticipate search engines, machine learning, or algorithmic recommendation. The memex was a tool for manual, deliberate curation — the researcher chose each link. There was no automation of discovery.

This distinction matters because contemporary invocations of the memex often project onto it capabilities Bush never described. The memex was not an artificial intelligence; it was a filing cabinet with superpowers.7

Conclusion

The memex remains a generative concept precisely because it is incomplete. Its emphasis on personal curation, associative trails, and information density continues to inform the design of research tools, even as the technological substrate has changed beyond recognition.

Future work might examine whether large language models represent a genuine departure from the memex paradigm or merely a new interface layer atop the same fundamental problem Bush identified: how to navigate more information than any human can hold in memory.
